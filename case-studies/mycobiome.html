Mycobiome Cancer Analysis

Background + Context

As a data scientist, I've always been interested in finding new ways to use data to solve important problems. One such problem is cancer detection. Every year, countless people are diagnosed with cancer, and getting an accurate and timely diagnosis can be critical in saving lives. So, when I heard about the idea that mycobiome data could be used to detect cancer, I was intrigued.

The mycobiome is the collection of fungal organisms that live within our bodies. The idea was that by analyzing these organisms, we could potentially detect the presence of cancer. A previous study had already analyzed whether mycobiome data could distinguish between multiple common cancer types and found that it could. However, my colleagues and I wanted to take this research further.

We had three main ideas. First, we wanted to investigate whether certain microbes were associated with late-stage cancer. If we could identify these microbes, we could potentially mute their effects to slow down or prevent cancer progression. Second, we wanted to find out whether certain microbes were associated with early-stage cancer or recovery. If we could identify these microbes, we could potentially introduce them into the tumor mycobiome to affect its development. Lastly, we wanted to see if mycobiome data could be used to accurately distinguish between cancer stages.

Problem Produces an Idea

However, we quickly ran into a problem. We discovered that fungal data alone wasn't very useful for distinguishing between cancer stages. There were too many cancer substages, and accuracy was too low. So, we formulated a hypothesis. If we consolidated cancer substages into the main four and reintroduced certain features from the metadata, we might be able to achieve high accuracy in predicting cancer stage.

Process

To test our hypothesis, we started by using the same dataset from the original analysis. We reintroduced metadata features such as age, sex, and sample type, and consolidated the cancer stages by mapping all substages to the parent stage. We also did some exploratory analysis and found that the dataset was slightly unbalanced, with certain cancer stages more represented than others. So, we subsetted our data by disease type and ran our models on each subset before generalizing our model accuracies into a weighted average.

Methods

For classification, we made a gradient boosting classifier with stratified 10-folds cross-validation using scikit-learn. For the gradient boost classifier, we used exponential loss, a learning rate of 0.1, n-estimators 150, and a max depth of 3. During the project, I primarily worked on preprocessing the data, which included initial data cleaning and setting up later model analysis.

Results

Our results were promising. We were able to predict cancer stage with high accuracy for stages 1 and 4 , but stages 2 and 3 were more muddled. We found that many of our metadata features were one-hot encoded, so the most important features in the analysis became each unique value in the metadata.

Conclusion

While our model accuracy was great, the relatively low scoring importance of the fungal features meant that further analysis would be needed to determine what microbes could be responsible for cancer progression. Nonetheless, our findings showed that by introducing creativity and ingenuity to a well-studied dataset, novel results were still possible. We hope that other scientists will see the work we've done and build upon it to expand cancer research.
